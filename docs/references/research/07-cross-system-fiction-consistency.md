# Cross-System Fiction Consistency Audit
**Date:** 2026-05-16  
**Status:** First pass — findings only, no fixes applied  
**Scope:** Narrative tone, lore coherence, world terminology, authoring standards

---

## What this looks at

The game is a grounded, morally complex narrative strategy game set in the city of Valdenmoor. The player manages House Valdris in a district called the Pale. The tone aims for political intrigue, domestic stakes, and moral ambiguity — not adventure fantasy, not comedy. This audit checks whether all authored text (events, NPC bios, activity log entries, ward origin stories, quest descriptions) stays within that tone and whether the world's terminology is consistent across all content sources.

---

## Critical findings

### C1 — House name contradicts the narrative bible

The canonical narrative document (`docs/narrative.md`) establishes the house name as **"House Valdris"** (10 references, including the founding history: "Gregor founded House Valdris roughly seventy years ago"). But data files and code use **"House Valdris"**:

- `data/definitions/npcs.json` — 12 references ("Holds a ledger that proves who profited from the fall of House Valdris")
- `data/definitions/events.json` — 1 reference ("On the morning the city learns House Valdris has an heir, four things happen before noon")
- `src/application/commands/pursuePlayerLegacy.ts:129` — heir birth originStory: "Born to \${npc.name} within House Valdris."

Two spellings for the player's own house will appear in the same game session.

**Fix:** Standardize to one spelling. The narrative bible is the canonical source — if it says "Valdris," update all data files and code strings to match. Or vice versa if "Valdris" is the intended name — update the narrative bible. Pick one and make it final.
**Fix** Use `docs/narrative.md` in the agents definition files as context

---

### C2 — Event references a non-existent district ID

An event in `data/definitions/events.json` uses `"sourceDistrictId": "district-pale-warrens"`. This district does not exist. The canonical district definitions include `"district-the-pale"` and `"district-the-warrens"` as separate entries — but not a combined `"district-pale-warrens"`.

The Pale and the Warrens are described as distinct neighborhoods with different social characters. The combined ID suggests authoring confusion about whether this event belongs to one or the other.

**Fix:** Change `"district-pale-warrens"` to either `"district-the-pale"` or `"district-the-warrens"` based on the event's narrative context. For a Tallow Ring broker event, `"district-the-pale"` (grey economy hub, Ring territory) is the appropriate fit.

## Medium findings

### M1 — "The Pale" used ambiguously — district vs. city

The narrative bible is clear: "the Pale" is a specific district inside the city of Valdenmoor. But casual event text sometimes uses "the Pale" in ways that blur this:

- "You decline. The broker nods and disappears into the Pale" — district reference, correct
- "The Pale keeps its silence" — unclear scope; reads as the whole city
- "A Pale Court operative" — faction naming potentially conflating the district with an institution

If a reader or future author does not have the narrative bible memorized, they may write "the Pale" as a synonym for the whole city, gradually diluting the setting's specificity.

**Fix:** Add a brief world glossary to the authoring documentation. Audit event text to replace city-scope uses of "the Pale" with "Valdenmoor" or the appropriate district name.

---

### M2 — Currency specificity drops in system-generated text

The authored events use "Marks" consistently and correctly (e.g., "Pay a goodwill bonus (50 Marks)"). The narrative bible defines a three-tier currency system: Marks (silver), Weights (gold), Tallies (credit documents). But activity log entries generated by commands use vague language:

- "You are nearly out of money" (generic)
- "The cost of running bonded labor settles into the house" (no amount, no unit)

The mechanical UI shows Marks correctly. The narrative flavor text lags behind.

**Fix:** When writing activity log entries in commands, use "Marks" rather than "money" or "coin." If an amount is known, include it: "Wages cost 24 Marks this week." Consider using a centralized mechanism for the currentcy.

---

### M3 — Ward birth originStory is functional but tonally thin

Biological heirs born via `tickPregnancyProgress` receive a generated `originStory`:
```
"Born to ${npc.name} within House Valdris."
```

This is a one-sentence placeholder. The setting's treatment of reproduction, legitimacy, and inheritance is one of its most morally charged areas (the Breeding Register, debt wards, legitimacy politics). A birth event that shapes the heir's entire narrative identity deserves more than a location stamp.

**Fix:** Author a small set of birth context templates (3–5 variants) keyed to the relationship's intimacy stage and social context:
- Committed relationship: "Born to \${name} in House Valdris, in the space between private will and public obligation."
- Unacknowledged: "The birth is registered. House Valdris does not yet name the child."
- Contested legitimacy: "Born to \${name}. The Merrow Registry has been notified."

## Low findings

### L1 — "Accept X" choice labels are mechanically phrased

Multiple event choices use the generic verb "Accept" where a fiction-specific verb would match the tone better:

- "Accept the meeting" — could be "Attend the meeting" or "Go to them"
- "Accept the supplies" — could be "Take the supplies" or "Receive the shipment"
- "Accept the treatment" — could be "Submit to treatment" or "Let them work"
- "Accept the arrangement" — could be "Agree to the terms" or "Sign the arrangement"

The narrative voice guide emphasizes "motive-driven, concrete language." "Accept" is transactional and passive — it describes what happens to the player, not what the player does.

**Fix:** Audit all event choice labels. Replace passive "Accept X" with active, context-specific verbs. Three words that describe what the player character is physically or socially doing are stronger than one generic verb.

---

### L2 — Guild Apprentice ward origin references an unconfirmed faction

The third ward origin story in `houseWard.ts:41–46` reads:

> "The Underhand Scribes offered an apprentice to learn letters and discretion inside a noble house. Useful to both parties."

"The Underhand Scribes" does not appear in the faction definitions, NPC bios, or event text anywhere else in the codebase. It may be a placeholder name. Compared to the other two ward origins (which ground the ward in established social dynamics — dock clearances, debt settlement), this one introduces an institution that has no backing lore.

**Fix:** Either (a) establish the Underhand Scribes as a named faction in the content definitions, or (b) replace this origin with one that references an already-established institution (e.g., the Gilded Court placing an apprentice, a minor Compact clerical posting, or a merchant guild connection).

---

### L3 — Bond service text is a positive model

`bondService.ts:153` and `bondService.ts:160` are cited here as positive examples:

> "Marion refuses to keep serving while bonded labor funds the house."

> "The cost of running bonded labor settles into the house. Empathic eyes do not look away."

These activity log entries embed moral weight into system feedback without editorializing. They follow the setting's rules (consequences are concrete, characters have positions) and show what good system text looks like. Future authors writing activity log entries in similar moral territory should follow this pattern.

### L4 — NPC backgrounds are a positive model

The authored NPC biographies in `data/definitions/npcs.json` are strong: specific, grounded, each one revealing role, history, and friction in two or three sentences. Marion Vale, Ida Rhys, and Verek Holst are cited examples. These demonstrate the authoring standard the setting requires. System-generated text (ward births, heir progressions, stage advancement messages) should aim for the same specificity.
